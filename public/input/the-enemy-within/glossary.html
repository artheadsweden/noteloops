Glossary (for Extra material)

Short, plain-English notes for modern readers. Each entry includes a quick “how it appears in the book” line so it stays anchored in Danny’s world, not a textbook.

Mining and strike terms

Colliery

A coal mine and the buildings, yards, and systems around it.

How it appears in the book: the place that owns the rhythm of Grimethorpe, even when everyone pretends it doesn’t.

Coal face / face worker

The working edge where coal is cut. A face worker works that seam directly.

How it appears in the book: pride, exhaustion, and a kind of expertise outsiders never see.

Deputy

An underground supervisor responsible for safety and oversight in a section.

How it appears in the book: authority that can protect you or sell you out, depending on the person.

Welfare / miners’ welfare

The miners’ social club and community centre, used for meetings, fundraising, and community life.

How it appears in the book: where arguments become votes, where grief becomes tea and forms.

Pit tally disc

A numbered disc used to track who is underground; in the novel it becomes a personal token.

How it appears in the book: Danny wears it like a weight and a vow.

NUM (National Union of Mineworkers)

The miners’ union, organising strike action, meetings, and support networks.

How it appears in the book: branch meetings, speeches, rows, loyalty, rupture.

NCB (National Coal Board)

The state body that ran the coal industry (management, closures, policy delivery).

How it appears in the book: the letters, the threats, the language that tries to sound “reasonable”.

Branch / branch secretary

Local union unit and its elected admin figure.

How it appears in the book: minutes, votes, bitter procedural fights that still matter.

Picket / picket line

A protest line outside a workplace or site, aimed at persuading or preventing entry.

How it appears in the book: cold mornings, flasks, chants, sudden fear.

Scab

A strikebreaker; an insult with real social consequences in strike communities.

How it appears in the book: said like a wound, not a slogan.

Orgreave

A major strike flashpoint centred on the Orgreave coking plant in South Yorkshire, associated with violent confrontations and lasting controversy.

How it appears in the book: shorthand for what the state is willing to do when it’s watched.

“Enemy within”

A political phrase used to frame internal dissent (especially militant unions) as a threat to the nation.

How it appears in the book: spat back as identity, turned into fuel.

Anti-nuclear, peace, and wider movement terms

CND (Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament)

A long-running UK peace movement campaigning against nuclear weapons.

How it appears in the book: badges on jackets, a shared language of protest across causes.

Greenham Common

RAF Greenham Common in Berkshire, and especially the women-led peace camp that became an emblem of 1980s anti-nuclear protest.

How it appears in the book: posters on walls, “Greenham women” turning up with tactics and hard-earned calm.

Peace camp

A long-term protest encampment outside a military or state site, built to make opposition physically unavoidable.

How it appears in the book: a network as much as a place, people who’ve learned endurance.

Cruise missiles

Cold War-era US missiles stationed in Western Europe; a major focus of UK protest movements in the 1980s.

How it appears in the book: poster text, pub arguments, a background dread that never quite leaves.

Reagan / Gorbachev

Leaders of the US and USSR whose rivalry and negotiations shaped late Cold War politics.

How it appears in the book: names dropped like weather, the world pressing in on local lives.

Politics, parties, and labels

Tories

Colloquial term for the Conservative Party.

How it appears in the book: said with anger, sometimes with reluctant precision.

MP

Member of Parliament.

How it appears in the book: Charlotte’s family world, and the sense of decisions made far away.

Thatcher

Margaret Thatcher, UK Prime Minister 1979–1990, central figure in the miners’ strike and Poll Tax era.

How it appears in the book: a name that functions like a pressure point.

Militant

A Trotskyist tendency associated with entryism inside parts of the Labour movement (especially in the 1980s).

How it appears in the book: muttered at meetings, suspicion, factional heat.

Red Wedge

A late-1980s music and arts campaign supporting Labour and anti-Conservative organising.

How it appears in the book: a sign that pop culture and politics are starting to braid together.

National Front (NF)

Far-right British party active in street politics, especially late 1970s into the 1980s.

How it appears in the book: a threat in the background, the reason some gigs feel like front lines.

Rock Against Racism

A cultural movement using gigs and festivals to oppose racism and far-right organising.

How it appears in the book: flyers, gig folklore, the idea that music can be a shield and a megaphone.

Section 28 / Clause 28

A law (Local Government Act 1988) that banned the “promotion” of homosexuality by local authorities, widely seen as state-sponsored stigma.

How it appears in the book: fear in the margins, coded talk, sudden silences, rage that can’t always be said aloud.

Poll Tax, courts, and enforcement terms

Community Charge (Poll Tax)

The official name for the Poll Tax: a flat per-person local tax that sparked mass opposition.

How it appears in the book: court corridors, brown envelopes, a system designed to grind people down.

Arrears

Overdue unpaid amounts.

How it appears in the book: a word that turns into a threat.

Summons

A legal notice ordering someone to attend court.

How it appears in the book: the moment “ignore it” stops being an option.

Magistrates’ court

Lower court dealing with high-volume cases, including unpaid local charges.

How it appears in the book: conveyor-belt justice, waiting, fluorescent light.

Magistrate

A lay judge (or bench) deciding summary matters.

How it appears in the book: bored power, brisk decisions, occasional human flickers.

Clerk

Court official handling procedure, paperwork, and the pace of hearings.

How it appears in the book: the gatekeeper to delays, wording, and small tactical wins.

Adjournment

A postponement to another date.

How it appears in the book: a pressure-release valve, sometimes a tactic.

Legal aid

State-funded legal support for people who can’t afford representation.

How it appears in the book: limited help, uneven access, a system with holes.

Ombudsman

An independent official who investigates complaints about maladministration.

How it appears in the book: a lever you only get if you know it exists.

Bailiffs

Enforcement agents who can seize goods to recover debts after court orders.

How it appears in the book: fear made physical, the knock that changes a house.

Warrant sale

A Scottish enforcement process involving warrants and sale of goods (distinct terminology from England).

How it appears in the book: cross-border learning, tactics travelling faster than authorities expect.

Sheriff officers

Scottish enforcement officers involved in serving documents and executing court orders.

How it appears in the book: proof the movement is UK-wide, not just London and Yorkshire.

Remission (discretionary remission)

A reduction or write-off mechanism for charges or arrears, usually hardship-based and not volunteered loudly.

How it appears in the book: the kind of thing Shell teaches people to ask for.

Hardship

A recognised state of inability to pay, used in applications and arguments.

How it appears in the book: people forced to turn their private lives into evidence.

Movement infrastructure (your in-world tools)

The Furnace

A community arts and organising hub, built to be useful as well as loud.

How it appears in the book: meetings, builds, rotas, a place that makes people harder to isolate.

Furnace FM

A pirate/independent radio voice linked to the collective.

How it appears in the book: signal, reach, the thrill of speaking without permission.

Women Against Pit Closures (WAPC)

Women-led support and organising network formed around the strike.

How it appears in the book: fundraising, logistics, steel in ordinary life.

Five Fingerprints

Danny’s framework for how protest becomes workable: Noise, Base, Movement, Access, Disruption.

How it appears in the book: not theory, but a set of things they build and repeat.

Coach grid

A structured plan for transport to actions (routes, seats, pickup points, backups).

How it appears in the book: spreadsheets before spreadsheets, done by hand.

Stewards

Volunteer marshals who manage crowd safety and flow at events and marches.

How it appears in the book: bibs, briefings, calm voices under pressure.

Music, media, and DIY production terms

100 Club

A real London venue (Oxford Street) associated with punk, reggae, and underground gigs.

How it appears in the book: sweat, proximity, the feeling that something is being recorded into history.

John Peel / Peel Session

John Peel was a BBC Radio One DJ known for championing new music; a Peel Session is an in-studio recording for his show.

How it appears in the book: a doorway into a wider audience, and a fight about what “success” costs.

Radio One

BBC’s major popular music radio station (especially influential in the 1980s).

How it appears in the book: the mainstream gate you can sometimes pick, sometimes kick.

Top of the Pops

BBC TV chart show with enormous cultural reach.

How it appears in the book: the dream and the joke, depending on who’s talking.

NME

New Musical Express, a major UK music paper that shaped scenes and reputations.

How it appears in the book: reviews that can bless, flatten, or misunderstand.

Zine

DIY magazine made by fans/activists, often photocopied and stapled.

How it appears in the book: messy typography, sharp opinions, hand-to-hand distribution.

Portastudio

A cassette-based multi-track recorder used for home demos.

How it appears in the book: hiss, late nights, the sound of making do.

Demo

A rough recording made to show a band’s sound.

How it appears in the book: something you post, something you trade, something you hide pride inside.

EP

Extended Play record: longer than a single, shorter than an album.

How it appears in the book: a milestone they can afford before the world takes notice.

PA

Public address system for amplifying vocals/instruments at gigs.

How it appears in the book: feedback, shouted instructions, the sound of a room changing shape.

Soundcheck

Pre-gig test of levels and equipment.

How it appears in the book: arguments about balance, and small rituals of control.

C90

A 90-minute cassette tape, cheap and ubiquitous.

How it appears in the book: copied, labelled, passed around like contraband.

Inlay

The paper insert in a cassette case, used for track lists and messaging.

How it appears in the book: cut by hand, scrawled on, a tiny billboard.

Letraset

Rub-down lettering sheets used for DIY design before home printers were common.

How it appears in the book: imperfect alignment, the look of effort.

Jiffy bag

Padded mailing envelope.

How it appears in the book: tapes and paperwork travelling cheaply and quietly.

Bootleg

An unofficial recording, usually of a live performance.

How it appears in the book: the gig you can relive, with all the flaws intact.

Dub / dubbing

Copying audio/video from one tape to another.

How it appears in the book: duplication as strategy, not piracy.

Master cassette

The original tape used to duplicate copies.

How it appears in the book: guarded like a crown jewel.

Gaffer tape

Heavy cloth tape used to fix, bind, and tame cables.

How it appears in the book: patch it, hold it, get through the night.

VHS

Home video format used for recording, copying, and evidence.

How it appears in the book: proof that doesn’t care what the headlines say.

Timecode

The hours:minutes:seconds reference used to locate moments on video tapes.

How it appears in the book: Mickey writing numbers that become ammunition.

TSQ labels

Danny’s shorthand on VHS spines for Trafalgar Square tapes (for example TSQ–NORTH–A, ARRESTS–EAST).

How it appears in the book: an evidence library built in real time.

Betamax

An early home video format often mentioned alongside VHS.

How it appears in the book: a wrong guess, a reminder that formats die even when truth doesn’t.

BBC owl

A nickname for the BBC mark as it appears on a pass/badge, described in a way that makes it feel like an “owl” watching the room.

How it appears in the book: the polished observers at the back, deciding what your life “means”.

Places and lived-in terms

Bedsit

A single-room rented flat (bed/sitting room combined), common in cramped city life.

How it appears in the book: cheap heat, shared walls, the sound of other people’s lives.

Council estate / scheme

Estate: local-authority housing; scheme is a common Scottish term for the same idea.

How it appears in the book: community, judgement from outsiders, and solidarity that comes with shared stairwells.

Dole

Informal term for unemployment benefits.

How it appears in the book: the line between surviving and vanishing.

Giro

Benefit payment, historically associated with girocheques and payday queues.

How it appears in the book: money that arrives with stigma attached.

Nowt / owt

Northern English for nothing / anything.

How it appears in the book: speech that pins the story to place.

Wifie

Scots term (often affectionate) for a woman, usually older.

How it appears in the book: solidarity spoken in regional language.

Copper / filth

Slang for police (filth is more openly hostile).

How it appears in the book: shouted in crowds, muttered in corridors, a reflex of lived experience.

Kettle / kettling

A crowd-control tactic where police contain protesters within a cordon for long periods.

How it appears in the book: hours of waiting, cold limbs, tempers that don’t drain away.

Truncheon / baton

Police stick used in public-order policing.

How it appears in the book: a word that changes how safe a street feels.
